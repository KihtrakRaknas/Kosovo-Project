What is Kosovo?

Kosovo location on the map (highlighted in red)

In 1998 Kosovo was a province in the southern part of Serbia. The genocide, or “ethnic cleansing” as it was called at the time, occured in Kosovo from 1998 to 1999.

Prejudice/Hatred

Battle of Kosovo


A painting depicting the battle

The tension between two ethnic groups, the Serbs and Muslim Albanians, stems all the way back from the 1389 Battle of Kosovo. The battle took place in Kosovo and was fought between an army led by Serbian Prince Lazar Hrebeljanović defending from the invading army of the Ottoman empire. The Serbs lost the battle and Lazar died fighting, but Lazar was venerated as a saint and the day of this battle is an important part of Serb national and ethnic identity. This is where the underlying hatred Serbians have for invaders and Muslim Albanians comes from.

Serbian Prince Lazar Hrebeljanović


Tension Leading to the Conflict


IDK what this is

Ethnic Albanians control Kosovo and were the third largest ethnic group at the time of the conflict. Yugoslavia defines Albanians as a “nationality”, not an autonomous nation within, and yet Serbs, Croats, Bosnians, Slovenes, and Macedonians are not classified this way. Serbia viewed Kosovo’s autonomy as threat to Serbian sovereignty and its interests, worrying that it diluted their power. The poverty occuring in Kosovo does not help to ease tensions, either. Because Albanians were a majority, Serbs and other minorities were sometimes subject to harassment or discrimination, and even violence. When Slobodan Milosevic becomes president of the Serbian republic in 1989, he places Kosovo under the control of the Serbian capital. Milosevic and the Serbian minority in Kosovo had long objected to the fact that Muslim Albanians controlled an land they considered sacred. Needless to say, Kosovar Albanians are not happy about their loss of autonomy and resist Serbian control.



Victims/Perpetrators/Methods

The KLA initiated the conflict and violence, and the Serbian forces perpetuated and accelerated the violence during the Kosovo conflict. Atrocities and war crimes were committed on both sides. Not even civilians were safe from these atrocities.



KLA


The Kosovo Liberation Army(KLA) is created in 1996, and begins attacks on Serbian police and politicians. The violence gradually escalated, and by 1998, the KLA’s attacks could be considered a substantial armed uprising. One example of a particularly brutal incident occurred in September 1998: KLA forces murdered thirty-four people near Glodjane. The local commanders must have ordered it or at the very least known about the mass murder.



Yugoslav Army/Serbian Police


Yugoslav and Serbian forces responded to the KLA’s attacks with a ruthless counteroffensive and a program of what they called “ethnic cleansing” - essentially, genocide. Serbian forces drove out all of Kosovo’s ethnic Albanians, displacing hundreds of thousands of people. In 1998 alone the Serbian military and police conflict with Kosovar Albanians killed 1,500 Albanians and displaced 400,000. What would happen is that the army and police surrounded a village, then shelled it from a distance. Horror then manifested in the form of mass rapes, beatings, tortures, mutilizations, and killings, all meant to force victims from their homes. The remaining villagers were gathered in one place while the police stole personal possessions from them then looted and burned the remains of the village. The men were separated from women and interrogated about the KLA - those suspected of ties with the KLA were executed. Women, children, and the elderly were often expelled. The Serbs then claimed the “cleansed” area as their own. Thousands of Kosovar Albanians fled from their homes. The event that finally caused the international community to take action occurred on January 15, 1999. Serb-allied forces murdered 45 Albanian civilians at Racak, mutilated most of the bodies, then attempted to cover of the horrific event. Widespread media coverage of the massacre enraged the international community.
